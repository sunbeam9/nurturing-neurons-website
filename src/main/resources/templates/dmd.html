Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy

Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD) is a rare X-linked genetic disorder affecting approximately 1 in 3,500 male children worldwide. Symptoms are first noticed during early childhood between the ages of three and six years. Numerous variations and related conditions make up the wide spectrum of conditions classified as muscular dystrophies that affect approximately 250,000 individuals in the U.S.Â  All muscular dystrophies affect the muscles of the body and differ largely in the type of muscles they affect and the severity of the disease. DMD is a progressive disease that causes gradual weakness and wasting of muscles throughout the body resulting in patients needing the support of a wheelchair by the time they are teenagers. Some patients go on to developing serious or life threatening complications including conditions related to the heart, lungs and digestive system. In some patients the ensuing respiratory conditions may result in the need of a ventilatory to assist with breathing.

Symptoms

Children with DMD may present with delays in reaching developmental milestones, particularly those involved with walking and posture. Abnormalities in the curvature of the spine, muscle contractures, bone fractures, and frequent falling are not uncommon. By the age of eight patients frequently require leg braces to help them walk and by the age of twelve most patients are in the wheelchair. One third of patients with DMD display cognitive impairment with learning disabilities, attention deficit and autisitic spectrum disorder.

Diagnosis

A diagnosis can be challenging and is based on family history, symptoms, and clinical exams.

Treatment

Currently there is no cure for DMD and treatment is aimed at symptom management. Corticosteroids and some drugs such as eteplirsen, deflazacort, golodiresen and vitolarsen have been used to help DMD patients. Physical therapy, assistive tools and mechanical aids such as braces, canes and wheelchairs are used to support patients.
